Launching A New Edition

Background

Houghton Mifflin was preparing to introduce its new American Heritage Dictionary into the marketplace. In previous years they had consistently used publishing agencies to bring new products to market, but they felt some new "out-of-the-box" thinking was needed to win the potential sales they felt this dictionary could generate.

Our target: Students and the other dictionary owners who rarely (after 10 years) if ever purchase a new edition, as well as writers, avid readers, academicians and graduation gift-givers.

Strategy, Positioning, Execution

Our strategy: to sell at least 250,000 copies in the year of introduction (Summer '92-'93); create a brand identity for American Heritage that would carry through to derivative products; and create a marketing platform for ongoing sales beyond the year of introduction.

Our positioning to consumers presented a life-style message - that as times change, words change too; and if you don't want to be obsolete in the way you use language, your dictionary shouldn't be obsolete either.

Execution included jacket design, sales materials, brochure, binders, t-shirts, point-of-purchase, trade and consumer advertising.

Results
The new edition is the only dictionary to have made the New York Times "Best-Seller List" - and it remained there for six consecutive weeks.
